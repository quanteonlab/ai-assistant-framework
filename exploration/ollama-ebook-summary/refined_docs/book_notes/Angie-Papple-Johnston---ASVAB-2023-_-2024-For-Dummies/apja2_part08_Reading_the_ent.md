#### Figuring Out What the Question is Asking
Background context explaining that determining exactly what the question is asking is crucial, as sometimes the question might be indirectly stated. Clue words can help identify the nature of the required calculation (e.g., addition: sum, total; division: share, ratio).

:p How do you determine what a word problem is asking for?
??x
Identify and focus on the specific question being asked in the problem. Sometimes, the question might not be directly stated but can be inferred from the context or clue words. Common clue words include:
- Addition: sum, total, in all, perimeter, increased by, combined, added
- Division: share, distribute, ratio, quotient, average, per, out of, percent
- Equals: is, was, are, were, amounts to
- Multiplication: product, total, area, cubic, times, multiplied by, of
- Subtraction: difference, how much more, exceed, less than, fewer than, decreased

The problem might be indirectly stated, so look for these clues to understand the actual question.

#### Digging for the Facts
Background context explaining that after identifying what the question is, you need to determine which data are relevant and which are not. List all facts provided in the problem with their units of measurement.

:p How do you identify relevant information in a word problem?
??x
List all the given information and variables from the problem on your scratch paper. Attach any units of measurement (e.g., miles, feet, inches). Then, cross out or ignore data that are not relevant to the question at hand.

For example:
- Tom sold 15 candy bars
- Becky sold 12 candy bars
- Debbie sold 17 candy bars
- Jane sold the most at 50

The question is: How many candy bars did Becky, Debbie, and Jane sell? Therefore, Tom's sales are irrelevant and can be crossed out.

The relevant facts are:
- Becky 12 bars
- Debbie 17 bars
- Jane 50 bars

Summing the remaining bars gives you the answer.
