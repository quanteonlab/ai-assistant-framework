#### Contract Fraud vs. Tort Fraud

Background context: When a victim of fraud can prove that the other party intentionally misled her, she has two potential remedies—contract fraud and tort fraud. The choice between these remedies depends on the client's desired outcome and the language used in the contract.

If the contract says "Business B warrants that it has assets worth $300,000," then Business A can get damages if the warranty is not true. If the contract says, "Business B represents that it has assets worth $300,000," then Business A can avoid the contract if the representation is not true.

Many drafters use the phrase "warrants and represents" to preserve a choice of remedies.

:p What are the two potential remedies in fraud cases, and what do they entail?
??x
In contract law, if a victim proves that the other party intentionally misled her, she can pursue either contract fraud or tort fraud as remedies. Contract fraud involves avoiding the contract and being put back into the pre-contract position, whereas tort fraud allows affirming the contract while seeking damages, which may include punitive damages not allowed in contract law.

Contract fraud:
- Avoid the contract
- Be put back into the pre-contract position

Tort fraud:
- Affirm the contract (stay in it)
- Make a claim to recover damages, including potentially punitive damages

To preserve flexibility, many drafters use the phrase "warrants and represents" in contracts.

#### Duress Defense

The key elements of duress are:
- One party made an unlawful threat
- The threatened party had no reasonable alternative but to accept the terms
- The threat induced the contract formation

Duress claims more commonly arise from financial (economic) threats rather than physical ones. For example, a web developer may hold a client's website hostage to force renegotiation and payment of additional fees.

:p What are the four key elements required to qualify for a duress defense?
??x
The four key elements required to qualify for a duress defense are:
1. One party made an unlawful threat.
2. The threatened party had no reasonable alternative but to accept the terms being offered.
3. The threat induced the contract formation.
4. The party presenting the threat caused or threatened to cause the duress instead of simply taking advantage of it.

For example, a web developer holding a client's website hostage to force renegotiation and additional payment would be an economic duress case.

The statute of frauds originated from the Statute of Frauds enacted in England in 1677, which listed various transactions that required writing. This law was adopted by American jurisdictions with some variations. The rationale was to prevent fraudulent claims and ensure clarity in agreements involving significant financial or property interests. Over time, many states have adapted their own statutes, though the core principles remain similar.
