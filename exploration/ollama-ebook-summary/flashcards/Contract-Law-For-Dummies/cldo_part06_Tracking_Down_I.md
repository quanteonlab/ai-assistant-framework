#### Illusory Promises
Background context explaining that contracts bind both parties and if one party isn't bound by an agreement, it can be considered an illusory promise. Detailed explanation of why certain promises are illusory.

:p What defines an illusory promise?
??x
An illusory promise occurs when one party doesn’t promise the other anything legally meaningful, making both parties unbound by the agreement. This is often seen in scenarios where a choice is given but no real commitment to act is made.

For example, if you e-mail "For $10,000, I’ll either sell you my car or I won’t," and someone responds with an acceptance, there’s no enforceable promise because the seller isn't committed to selling the car. Both parties are unbound.

#### The Wood v. Lucy, Lady Duff-Gordon Case
In this famous case, Lady Duff-Gordon tried to escape her contract by arguing that Wood's promise was illusory because it did not include a specific effort or commitment from him. However, Judge Benjamin Cardozo ruled that the agreement implied reasonable efforts on Wood’s part.
:p What did Judge Cardozo determine in the Wood v. Lucy case?
??x
Judge Cardozo determined that even though the contract might be "imperfectly expressed," it should not be voided just because of a lack of explicit effort clause. He found that the agreement implied Wood's promise to use reasonable efforts, thereby providing consideration and making the contract enforceable.

#### Reliance: Acting or Refraining Based on Promises
Background context explaining the concept. Include any relevant formulas or data here.
Reliance occurs when one party acts or refrains from acting based on a promise made by another. Even if the promise is not enforceable as a contract, the promisor may still be legally obligated to compensate the promisee for losses incurred due to reliance.

For example, if A promises B that he will provide a place to live after B moves, and B incurs expenses in moving based on this promise, B can claim compensation under the doctrine of reliance.
:p What is relied upon in the context of reliance?
??x
In the context of reliance, one party acts or refrains from acting based on a promise made by another. If the promisor breaches the promise, even if it isn't enforceable as a contract, the promisee may still have a claim for compensation.

#### Restitution: Making a Party Disgorge Benefits
Background context explaining the concept. Include any relevant formulas or data here.
Restitution is an act of making one party disgorge (relinquish) a benefit when another party has conferred a benefit on them. The doctrine of restitution prevents unjust enrichment.

For instance, in Mills v. Wyman, Mills cared for Wyman's adult son without receiving anything in return. Mills can claim restitution from Wyman’s son for the value of the services provided.
:p What is restitution?
??x
Restitution involves making one party disgorge (relinquish) a benefit that was conferred on them by another party, to prevent unjust enrichment. It allows a party who has conferred a benefit to seek compensation or return of the benefit from the recipient.

#### Determining Formal Contracts vs. Fallback Doctrines
Background context explaining the concept. Include any relevant formulas or data here.
To determine if two parties have a formal contract or are legally obligated by fallback doctrines, follow these steps:

1. Look for a bargained-for contract with all conditions met: offer, acceptance, and consideration.
2. If no such contract exists, check for reliance where the promisee’s change in position incurs costs.
3. Consider restitution if one party has conferred a benefit on another without mutual agreement.
