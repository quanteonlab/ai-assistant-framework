#### Freedom of Contract Principle
Background context explaining the principle. This concept is foundational to contract law and allows individuals of legal age and sound mind to enter into contracts that are lawful. It forms the basis for most contractual relationships but can be challenged under certain circumstances.

:p What does freedom of contract entail in contract law?
??x
Freedom of contract refers to the fundamental principle that legally competent individuals have the right to freely negotiate and enter into agreements as long as they are not illegal or contrary to public policy. This means that parties can bargain for terms within allowable legal boundaries, and courts generally uphold such contracts unless specific defenses are raised.

#### Illegal Contracts
Contracts that violate laws, statutes, or regulations are considered illegal and therefore unenforceable. Engaging in activities that are unlawful through a contract nullifies its validity entirely.

:p Can you provide an example of an illegal contract?
??x
An example of an illegal contract is one where parties agree to engage in a criminal activity. For instance, if two individuals enter into a contract for the sale or purchase of drugs, which are illegal under federal and state laws, such a contract would be unenforceable because its subject matter violates the law.

#### Contracts Contrary to Public Policy
Contracts that go against societal norms, moral standards, or legal policies are deemed void. These contracts cannot be enforced by the courts due to their adverse impact on public welfare and order.

:p What defines a contract contrary to public policy?
??x
A contract is considered contrary to public policy if it violates established social values, moral principles, or legal statutes. For example, agreements that encourage discrimination, harm public health, or threaten national security are not enforceable because they contravene the general welfare and interests of society.

#### Unconscionable Terms
Unconscionable terms in a contract refer to clauses that are so one-sided as to be unfair. Courts may refuse to enforce such terms if they find them grossly unfair or oppressive, particularly when one party has an overwhelming advantage over the other.

:p Can you describe what constitutes unconscionable terms?
??x
Unconscionable terms are provisions in a contract that are so overly favorable to one party and disadvantageous to another that they shock the conscience of the court. For example, clauses that impose unfair penalties, excessive fees, or limit remedies for one party while providing no reciprocal protections would be considered unconscionable.

#### Lack of Capacity
A lack of capacity refers to situations where a person is unable to fully understand the nature and consequences of entering into a contract due to mental incapacity, intoxication, or other factors. Such contracts may be voided if it can be proven that the party lacked the necessary understanding at the time of agreement.

:p What constitutes lack of capacity in the context of contract law?
??x
Lack of capacity occurs when an individual is not capable of entering into a contract due to reasons such as mental incapacity, intoxication, or illness. For instance, minors (under legal age), individuals with mental disorders that impair judgment, or those under the influence of drugs or alcohol may lack the necessary capacity to enter legally binding agreements.

#### Fraud
Fraud in a contract involves intentional misrepresentation by one party to another, leading to detrimental reliance and resulting in an unfair advantage for the fraudulent party. Contracts obtained through fraud are voidable at the option of the deceived party.

:p How is fraud defined in contractual terms?
??x
Fraud in a contract occurs when one party intentionally deceives another with false statements or omissions that induce the other party to enter into a contract against their best interests. The deceived party can then seek to rescind the contract, recover damages, or both.

#### Duress and Undue Influence
Duress involves coercion by one party using threats of harm (actual or threatened) to force another party to agree to terms they would not otherwise accept. Undue influence occurs when a more powerful individual uses their position to unduly affect the will of another, leading them to enter into an agreement against their true intentions.

:p What is duress in the context of contract law?
??x
Duress in contract law involves situations where one party threatens to inflict harm or actual coercion on another to compel them to agree to a contract. The threatened harm can be physical or non-physical (e.g., economic, social) and must be significant enough to overbear the will of the victim.

#### Mistake
Mistakes in contracts occur when one or both parties hold incorrect beliefs about a fact that is crucial to the agreement's validity. These mistakes can be mutual, where both parties are mistaken, or unilateral, where only one party holds an incorrect belief. Contracts based on such mistakes may be voidable.

:p What constitutes a mistake in contract law?
??x
A mistake in contract law refers to circumstances where there is a factual error that significantly impacts the agreement's validity. This can happen if both parties (mutual mistake) or just one party (unilateral mistake) holds an incorrect belief about a crucial fact at the time of entering into the contract.

#### Statute of Frauds
The statute of frauds requires certain contracts to be in writing and signed by the party against whom enforcement is sought. Failure to comply with this requirement renders the agreement unenforceable unless there are exceptions provided by law.

:p What does the statute of frauds require?
??x
The statute of frauds mandates that specific types of contracts must be reduced to writing and signed by the party who is to perform the obligation alleged in the contract. This ensures that important agreements are documented and provides evidence for enforcement if necessary.

#### Principle of Fairness

#### U.S. Constitution and Contracts
Background context: The U.S. Constitution contains the Contract Clause, which prohibits states from impairing contract obligations. However, courts have interpreted this clause narrowly, focusing on preventing states from relieving individuals of their debt obligations.

:p What does the Contract Clause in the U.S. Constitution prevent?
??x
The Contract Clause in the U.S. Constitution prevents states from passing laws that impair contractual obligations. It was originally intended to protect against states relieving individuals of their debt obligations, particularly those owed to foreign creditors.
