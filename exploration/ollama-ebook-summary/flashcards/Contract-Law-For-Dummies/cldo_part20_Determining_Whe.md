This means that even though there is a minor deviation from the contract terms, the non-breaching party cannot refuse to fulfill their end of the agreement.

Explanation: This rule ensures fairness in contracts by preventing parties from avoiding their responsibilities due to small deviations or errors.

#### Creating Conditions vs. Promises in Contracts

When writing contracts, it is crucial to use clear language to define conditions and promises accurately. Using terms like "if" or "on condition that" creates a condition, while using "shall," "has an obligation to," or "agrees to" creates a promise.

:p How can you ensure your contract includes a conditional clause rather than a promissory one?
??x
To create a condition in a contract, use phrases like "if" or "on condition that." For example:
```contract
Contractor will pay Subcontractor when Owner pays Contractor.
```
This implies the payment is contingent on the owner's payment. However, this can be interpreted ambiguously by courts.

To create a promise, use terms such as "shall," "has an obligation to," or "agrees to." For example:
```contract
Contractor shall pay Subcontractor for all work performed.
```
This clearly states that the contractor is promising to pay regardless of any conditions. 

By using clear language, you can avoid misunderstandings and potential disputes.

Restitution involves requiring a party who has been unjustly enriched at another's expense to return the benefit received. This remedy is particularly useful when partial performance leads to nonpayment under the contract.

:p In what situation might restitution be applicable in contract law?

#### Cardozo's Interpretation of Terms
On appeal, Judge Cardozo interpreted terms partly based on justice and presumed intentions to determine whether a term was a promise or a condition. He concluded that reasonable parties would not have intended every single specification to be a strict condition.

:p How did Judge Cardozo interpret the meaning of the pipe clause?
??x
Judge Cardozo believed that it was unreasonable for Jacob & Youngs to be held strictly to the brand name Reading pipe. He argued that the term should be seen as a promise rather than an express condition, because reasonable parties wouldn't have intended every specification to be treated so rigidly.

#### Material vs. Immaterial Breach
Even if the pipe condition was a promise rather than a strict condition, Cardozo found that Jacob & Youngs materially breached their promise by using Cohoes pipe instead of Reading pipe.

:p What did Judge Cardozo determine about the breach?
??x
Judge Cardozo determined that Jacob & Youngs had materially breached their promise to use Reading pipe. A material breach excused Kent's performance, meaning Kent was no longer required to make the final payment until the condition (brand-specific pipe) was met.

#### Damages Awarded by Cardozo
Cardozo ruled that because Cohoes pipe served the same purpose as Reading pipe, Jacob & Youngs had acted innocently. Therefore, he awarded damages based on the difference in value between what Kent promised and what he got.

:p How did Judge Cardozo determine the amount of damages?
??x
Judge Cardozo determined that since Cohoes pipe was functionally equivalent to Reading pipe, there was no actual loss. Thus, he awarded Kent zero damages because the pipe served the same purpose, making it impossible for Kent to prove any monetary loss.

#### Concerns About the Decision
The dissent argued that this decision would allow contracting parties to cut corners and avoid fulfilling promises if they used equivalent materials. This could lead to a slippery slope of subpar performance.

:p What were the main concerns of the dissent in this case?
??x
The dissent was concerned that allowing contractors like Jacob & Youngs to use equivalent, though not identical, materials would encourage parties to cut corners and avoid fulfilling their promises. They feared this decision might create a precedent where contract terms could be loosely interpreted, leading to potential abuse.

#### Practical Lessons from the Case
From this case, contractors learned that they should either exclude specific brand names or clearly state that any brand meeting quality standards is acceptable. On the other hand, parties wanting something to be an express condition must spell it out explicitly.

:p What are the practical lessons learned from this legal decision?
??x
The key practical lessons include:
- Contractors: Don't promise a specific brand name; instead, use language like "any pipe of equivalent quality." 
- Parties who really want something to be a strict condition should clearly and unambiguously state it in the contract.
