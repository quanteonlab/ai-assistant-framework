#### Compare and Contrast: Picking Up on Signals
Background context explaining how to use signal words that indicate contrast or similarity. These signals can help you predict the meaning of an unknown word.

:p How do you use signal words like "indeed," "like," "too," "although," etc., in a sentence?
??x
Signal words can be very helpful in understanding the context and predicting the meaning of unfamiliar words. For example, if you see "as if" or "as though" in a sentence, it suggests that something is being compared to another thing.

For instance:
- They recoiled as if they had just seen a ghost.
  - This implies that "recoil" means a sudden and strong reaction of fear or shock. Common choices could be cringed (A), laughed (B), shouted (C), or endured (D). "Cringe" fits best because it matches the described reaction.

Signal words can also indicate contrast, which can help you identify incorrect options:
- Although she was hesitant, Cheryl voted to reopen the school.
  - Here, "although" indicates a contrast between two ideas. Since Cheryl is hesitating but still voting, "hesitant" and "voted" must be opposites or related in meaning.

By recognizing these signal words and their effects, you can often eliminate incorrect options and find the right answer more easily.
??x
