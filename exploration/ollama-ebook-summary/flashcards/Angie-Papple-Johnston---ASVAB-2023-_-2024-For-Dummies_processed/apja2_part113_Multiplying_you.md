#### Gears and Gear Series

Gears are commonly used to transmit motion between two shafts. They can change direction, increase or decrease speed, and increase force.

When gears are arranged in a series:
- If an even number of gears is connected, the first and last gear turn in opposite directions.
- If an odd number of gears is aligned, the first and last gear spin in the same direction.

The speed at which a gear rotates depends on the number of teeth it has. This can be expressed as a ratio:
$$\text{Speed Ratio} = \frac{\text{Number of Teeth on Driving Gear}}{\text{Number of Teeth on Driven Gear}}$$

For example, if Gear 1 with 6 teeth is connected to Gear 2 with 8 teeth, the speed ratio would be $\frac{6}{8}$, which simplifies to $\frac{3}{4}$.

#### Pulleys and Belt Systems

Pulleys can drive other pulleys when connected by a system of belts.

Turning direction: Pulleys connected in series rotate in the same direction unless the driving belt is reversed (twisted).

Speed of rotation: This depends on the diameter ratio:
$$\text{Speed Ratio} = \frac{\text{Diameter of Driven Pulley}}{\text{Diameter of Driving Pulley}}$$

For example, if Pulley A has a 1-inch diameter, Pulley B has a 2-inch diameter, and Pulley C has a 4-inch diameter, the speed ratios would be:
$$\frac{2}{1} = 2 \quad \text{(Pulley B rotates twice as fast as Pulley A)}$$
$$\frac{4}{2} = 2 \quad \text{(Pulley C rotates half as fast as Pulley B)}$$

Thus, for every full revolution of Pulley A, Pulley C makes a quarter of a revolution.
