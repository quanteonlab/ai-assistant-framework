#### Engine: Internal Combustion Process
Background context: An internal combustion engine burns fuel to produce mechanical energy, which powers a vehicle. This process involves multiple cycles where each cycle consists of four strokes.

:p How does an internal combustion engine convert chemical energy into mechanical energy?
??x
An internal combustion engine converts the air-fuel mixture's chemical energy into heat and then into mechanical energy through several strokes in a cycle. Hereâ€™s a step-by-step breakdown:

1. **Intake Stroke**: The intake valve opens, allowing a mixture of fuel and air to enter the cylinder as the piston moves downward.
2. **Compression Stroke**: As the piston moves upward, it compresses this mixture, increasing its temperature and pressure.
3. **Combustion Stroke**: A spark ignites the compressed mixture, causing an explosion that forces the piston down.
4. **Exhaust Stroke**: The exhaust valve opens, expelling the burnt gases as the piston moves back up.

Each cycle takes two rotations of the crankshaft to complete, meaning 4,800 rpm corresponds to 2,400 cycles per minute (assuming a tachometer reading).

#### Four-Stroke Engine
Background context: A four-stroke engine completes one cycle in four strokes, each stroke serving a specific purpose. The process is repeated multiple times to keep the engine running.

:p What are the four strokes of a typical internal combustion engine?
??x
The four strokes of a typical internal combustion engine are:
1. **Intake Stroke**: Introduces air-fuel mixture into the cylinder.
2. **Compression Stroke**: Compresses the air-fuel mixture to prepare for ignition.
3. **Combustion Stroke**: Ignites and expands the compressed mixture, forcing the piston down.

#### Fuel Injectors and Carburetors
Fuel injectors have replaced carburetors on newer cars, starting from the late 1950s but widely introduced by the late 1980s and early 1990s. The fuel injector acts as a nozzle that directly injects liquid fuel into the engine's air stream. This process typically requires an external pump.

In modern vehicles, electronic Fuel Injection Systems (EFI) are used, which receive commands from the Powertrain Control Module (PCM). The PCM uses data from sensors in the fuel, air, and exhaust systems to determine how much fuel is needed for optimal performance. A throttle controls the flow of fuel; it can be mechanically connected to a carburetor or electronically linked to an EFI system.

:p What are the key differences between fuel injectors and carburetors?
??x
Fuel injectors directly inject fuel into the air stream, require external pumps, and are controlled by electronic systems like EFI. Carburetors mix air and fuel outside the engine cylinder before it's drawn in.

#### Internal Combustion Engine Components
All internal combustion engines have similar components within the engine block, including pistons, piston rings, cylinders, cylinder heads, valves, chambers, and various shafts.

The **pistons** are cylindrical objects that move up and down in each cylinder. The **piston rings** seal the piston to the cylinder wall, preventing gases from escaping and stopping oil from entering the combustion chamber. The **cylinders** house the pistons and other components where the air-fuel mixture combusts. The **cylinder head** is located above the pistons; it contains the **combustion chambers**, as well as intake and exhaust valves.

The **intake valves** allow the air-fuel mix to enter the combustion chamber, while the **exhaust valves** release waste gases after combustion. The **crankshaft** converts the linear motion of the pistons into rotational motion. The **camshaft** operates the intake and exhaust valves in time with the engine's operations.

:p List the main components of an internal combustion engine.
??x
- Pistons: Move up and down in cylinders.
- Piston rings: Seal piston to cylinder wall, prevent gases from escaping, and keep oil out of the combustion chamber.
- Cylinders: Housing for pistons and other components; site of air-fuel mixture combustions.
- Cylinder head: Contains combustion chambers and intake/exhaust valves.
- Intake valves: Allow air-fuel mix into the combustion chamber.
- Exhaust valves: Release waste gases after combustion.
- Crankshaft: Converts piston motion to rotational movement.
- Camshaft: Operates intake and exhaust valves.

// Pseudocode for a basic engine component setup
public class Engine {
    private Cylinder[] cylinders;
    private Piston[] pistons;
    private CylinderHead cylinderHead;
    private Camshaft camshaft;
    private Crankshaft crankshaft;

    public void initialize() {
        // Initialize all components
        this.cylinders = new Cylinder[4]; // Example of 4-cylinder engine
        this.pistons = new Piston[4];
        this.cylinderHead = new CylinderHead();
        this.camshaft = new Camshaft();
        this.crankshaft = new Crankshaft();

        // Link components to function together
        for (int i = 0; i < cylinders.length; i++) {
            cylinders[i].attachPiston(pistons[i]);
            cylinders[i].attachCylinderHead(cylinderHead);
            cylinders[i].attachCamshaft(camshaft);
            cylinders[i].attachCrankshaft(crankshaft);
        }
    }

    public void startEngine() {
        // Start the engine by moving pistons up and down
        for (Piston piston : pistons) {
            piston.moveUp();
            piston.moveDown();
        }
    }
}
