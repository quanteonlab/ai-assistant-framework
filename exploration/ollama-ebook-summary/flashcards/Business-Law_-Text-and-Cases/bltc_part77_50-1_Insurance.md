#### Insurance Contracts and Terminology
Background context explaining the basic structure of an insurance contract. Key terms like policy, premium, and underwriter are introduced.

An **insurance contract** is called a *policy*, where the *consideration* paid to the insurer (the insurance company) is known as a *premium*. The *insurance company* is sometimes referred to as an *underwriter*. The parties involved in an insurance policy include the *insurer* and the *insured*.

Insurance contracts are typically obtained through either an **agent** or a **broker**. An agent usually works for the insurance company, whereas a broker operates independently. When dealing with an applicant, a broker acts as their representative (not the insurer's), while an insurance agent represents the insurer.

Agents owe *fiduciary duties* to the insurance company and not to the person applying for insurance. Generally, the insurance company is bound by the actions of its agents when they act within the scope of their authority.

:p What are the key terms used in an insurance contract?
??x
The key terms include:
- **Policy**: The insurance contract itself.
- **Premium**: Consideration paid to the insurer.
- **Underwriter**: Another term for the insurance company.
- **Insurer and Insured**: The parties involved, where the insured is covered by the policy's provisions.

These terms form the foundational language of insurance agreements.

#### Insurable Interest
Background context explaining the concept of insurable interest, which is crucial for enforceability. Without an insurable interest, there can be no valid insurance contract.

A person must have a *reasonable expectation of benefit* from the continued existence or use of something (insurable interest) to validly insure it. This interest must exist at the time the policy is obtained.

Insurance policies are based on the principle that the insured party has an insurable interest in the subject matter of the insurance.

:p What is the concept of insurable interest?
??x
The concept of **insurable interest** refers to a reasonable expectation of benefit from the continued existence or use of something. Without it, there can be no valid insurance contract because the transaction would essentially be treated as a wager without any underlying mutual agreement. 

For example:
- In life insurance, an individual must have a reasonable expectation of benefit from another person's continued life to have an insurable interest in that person’s life.
- This interest must exist at the time the policy is obtained.

Having an insurable interest ensures the enforceability and legitimacy of the insurance contract.

#### Risk Management and Insurance
Background context explaining that risk management involves transferring certain risks to an insurer through a contract. Focus on the idea of predicting potential loss based on known and unknown factors.

Risk can be described as a prediction concerning potential loss, based on both known and unknown factors. Managing risk often involves individuals or companies transferring specific risks to insurers in exchange for financial compensation if losses occur.

Risk management typically involves:
1. Identifying potential sources of loss.
2. Assessing the probability and impact of these losses.
3. Deciding whether to retain the risk or transfer it through insurance contracts.

Insurance is a key tool in this process, providing compensation for various types of losses including injury, death, property damage, and legal liabilities.

:p What are the main components of risk management?
??x
The main components of risk management include:
1. **Identifying Potential Sources of Loss**: Determining what might cause harm or financial loss.
2. **Assessing Probability and Impact**: Evaluating how likely a particular loss is to occur and its potential severity.
3. **Deciding Whether to Retain the Risk or Transfer it through Insurance Contracts**: Choosing between managing risk internally or transferring it by purchasing insurance.

These steps help individuals and businesses plan for and mitigate risks effectively.

#### Limited-Payment Life Insurance

#### Term Insurance
Term insurance provides coverage only during a specified term or period. If death occurs within this term, the insurer makes a payment to the beneficiary; otherwise, no payment is made. Premiums are lower compared to other types of life insurance, but there is usually no cash surrender value.
:p What is term insurance?
??x
Term insurance offers coverage for a specific period and pays out if the insured dies within this term. The premiums are lower than those in whole life or limited-payment life insurance, with no cash surrender value upon expiration.

#### Endowment Insurance
Endowment insurance involves fixed premium payments over a definite term. At the end of the term, a fixed amount is paid to the insured or, if they die during the specified period, to their beneficiary. This type has a rapidly increasing cash surrender value but comes with high premiums.
:p What is endowment insurance?
??x
Endowment insurance requires fixed premium payments for a specific term. Upon completion of this term, regardless of whether the insured is still alive, a fixed amount is paid out. Premiums are high due to the payment made at the end of the term.

#### Universal Life Insurance
Universal life insurance combines elements of term and whole life insurance. Each premium payment (called a "contribution") has two deductions: one for term insurance protection, and another for company expenses and profit. The remaining funds earn interest at a variable rate, providing a cash value.
:p What is universal life insurance?
??x
Universal life insurance blends aspects of term and whole life insurance. Premium contributions are used to cover term insurance costs and company expenses, with the remainder earning interest at a variable rate. This policy offers flexibility in premium payments and cash value growth.
