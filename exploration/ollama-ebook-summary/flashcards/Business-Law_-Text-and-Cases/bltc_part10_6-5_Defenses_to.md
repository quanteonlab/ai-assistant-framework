#### Proximate Cause and Foreseeability
In tort law, a defendant may be held liable for harm caused to another if their actions are the proximate cause of that harm. This means the defendant’s conduct must both have caused the harm (causation in fact) and be deemed a legal cause of the injury (proximate cause). The concept of foreseeability is central; the defendant's actions must create a foreseeable risk of injury for liability to arise.
:p What does the principle of proximate cause require in terms of causality?
??x
The principle of proximate cause requires that the defendant’s actions be both the factual cause (causation in fact) and a legal cause (proximate cause) of the harm. This means the defendant must have caused the injury, but also that this outcome was foreseeable.

#### Comparative Negligence in Detail
Background context explaining comparative negligence in more detail, including how it affects damages calculation.

Comparative negligence is a legal principle that reduces the amount of damages awarded based on the plaintiff’s degree of fault. In this case, if Elaine was partly at fault for the accident and her injuries, the jury might reduce her compensation accordingly.

:p Under what theory might her damages be reduced in proportion to the degree to which her actions contributed to the accident and her resulting injuries?
??x
Elaine's damages could be reduced under the comparative negligence theory. This means that if the jury finds Elaine was partly responsible for the accident, its judgment on the total damages would take into account her share of fault.

For instance, if the jury determines Elaine is 20% at fault and the total damages are $10,000, she would only receive $8,000 ($10,000 - 20% of $10,000).

The formula to calculate reduced damages due to comparative negligence can be expressed as:

\[ \text{Reduced Damages} = \left(1 - \frac{\text{Plaintiff's Fault Percentage}}{100}\right) \times \text{Total Damages} \]

In this case:
- Plaintiff’s Fault Percentage = 20%
- Total Damages = $10,000

Thus,
\[ \text{Reduced Damages} = \left(1 - \frac{20}{100}\right) \times \$10,000 = 0.8 \times \$10,000 = \$8,000 \]

This ensures that the plaintiff receives compensation proportional to their fault.

#### Strict Liability Overview
Background context: The modern concept of strict liability is based on an English case, Rylands v. Fletcher, decided in 1868. This doctrine holds that a person who engages in certain activities can be held responsible for any harm caused to others, even if they used the utmost care.
:p What is strict liability?
??x
Strict liability refers to situations where a defendant is liable for damages proximately caused by their actions, regardless of fault or negligence. In cases involving abnormally dangerous activities, such as blasting or storing explosives, the person engaged in the activity is responsible for any harm that results, even if they exercised reasonable care.
