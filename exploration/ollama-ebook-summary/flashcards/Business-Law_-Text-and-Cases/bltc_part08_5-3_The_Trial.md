#### Rules of Evidence
Background context: The rules of evidence determine whether evidence will be admitted in court. These rules ensure that any evidence presented is fair and reliable, with a focus on relevance and the reliability of the testimony. Relevant evidence must be directly related to the issues at hand, while hearsay (testimony about out-of-court statements) is generally not admissible.
:p What are the criteria for admitting evidence in court?
??x
The criteria for admitting evidence in court include:
1. **Relevance**: The evidence must be relevant to the matter in question and tend to prove or disprove a fact.
2. **Reliability**: The evidence should be reliable, meaning it can be verified through cross-examination or other means.
3. **Hearsay Exclusion**: Hearsay is generally not admissible unless an exception applies (e.g., declarations against interest).
??x
The criteria for admitting evidence ensure that the presented information is pertinent and trustworthy to support the claims made in a case. This helps maintain the integrity of the judicial process.

After the jury has rendered its verdict, either party may make a posttrial motion. The prevailing party typically requests that the court enter judgment according to the verdict. Conversely, the nonprevailing party often files specific motions such as a motion for a new trial or a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict (N.O.V.).

1. **Motion for a New Trial** - This is filed by the losing party to request that the court set aside the adverse verdict and hold a new trial.
2. **Motion for Judgment N.O.V. (Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict)** - Filed by the prevailing party if the judge believes the jury's decision was unreasonable or erroneous.
