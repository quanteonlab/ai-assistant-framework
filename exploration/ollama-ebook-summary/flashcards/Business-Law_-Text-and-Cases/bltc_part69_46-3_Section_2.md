#### Resale Price Maintenance Agreements
Background context: A resale price maintenance agreement (RPM) is an arrangement where a manufacturer sets the retail prices that its distributors or retailers can charge for its products. Historically, RPMs were considered per se violations under Section 1 of the Sherman Act but are now analyzed under the rule of reason.

:p What is a resale price maintenance agreement?
??x
A resale price maintenance agreement (RPM) is an arrangement between a manufacturer and its distributors or retailers where the manufacturer dictates the retail prices that the distributors or retailers can charge for the products. In the past, RPMs were deemed per se violations under Section 1 of the Sherman Act. However, today they are evaluated using the rule of reason.

The rule of reason considers both positive and negative aspects of RPMs:
- Positive: Setting maximum prices can sometimes increase competition by ensuring that retailers do not engage in excessive price-cutting.
- Negative: Such agreements may stifle competition if they prevent retailers from offering competitive pricing or discounts.

For minimum RPMs, which set the lowest acceptable retail prices, courts also apply a rule of reason analysis to determine their competitive impact.

#### Intent Requirement in Monopolization Cases
Background context: Monopoly power alone does not constitute a violation under Section 2 of the Sherman Act. The offense requires an intent to monopolize, meaning that acquiring market dominance must be achieved through anticompetitive means.

Good business practices, superior products, or historical accidents can lead to a dominant position without violating the law. However, if a firm uses anticompetitive tactics like price fixing, predatory pricing, or exclusionary conduct to gain and maintain monopoly power, it is in violation of the act.

:p What additional requirement must be met for acquiring monopoly power to constitute an antitrust violation under Section 2 of the Sherman Act?
??x
Intent to monopolize must be present. Acquiring market dominance through anticompetitive means constitutes a violation, whereas good business practices or historical accidents do not.

#### Cost Justification for Price Discrimination
Cost justification allows a seller to avoid liability for price discrimination by demonstrating that specific purchases saved the seller costs. If a particular buyer’s purchase reduces the overall cost of production and selling, the seller can argue that this is not discriminatory pricing but rather a justified reduction.
:p Can you explain how cost justification works in avoiding price discrimination?
??x
Cost justification involves showing that offering a lower price to one buyer saves the seller significant costs. This could be due to bulk discounts, reduced shipping costs, or other economies of scale. For instance, if a large retailer buys products at a discounted rate and passes on some of these savings to consumers while maintaining profitability, this is not considered discriminatory pricing.
```java

        // Example calculation: bulk discount from supplier
        int regularPrice = 100;
        int quantityDiscounted = 500;
        double totalCostWithoutDiscount = regularPrice * quantityDiscounted;
        double bulkDiscountRate = 0.1; // 10% discount for large orders
        double totalCostWithDiscount = (regularPrice - (regularPrice * bulkDiscountRate)) * quantityDiscounted;
        
        System.out.println("Total cost without bulk discount: " + totalCostWithoutDiscount);
        System.out.println("Total cost with bulk discount: " + totalCostWithDiscount);
    }
}
```

#### Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)
Background context explaining the HHI. The HHI is a formula used to measure market concentration by summing the squared market shares of all firms in a market.
The HHI is calculated as:
$$\text{HHI} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i^2$$where $ s_i$ represents the market share of firm i, and n is the total number of firms.

:p How is the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) calculated?
??x
The HHI is calculated by:
$$\text{HHI} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i^2$$where $ s_i$ represents the market share of each firm, and n is the total number of firms in the market.

For example, if there are two firms with 40% and 60% market shares:
$$\text{HHI} = (0.4)^2 + (0.6)^2 = 0.16 + 0.36 = 0.52$$
The HHI is often expressed as a percentage.
