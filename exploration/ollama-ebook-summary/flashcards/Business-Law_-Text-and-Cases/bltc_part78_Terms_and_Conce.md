#### Testate vs. Intestate

A person who dies with a valid will is said to have died **testate**. Conversely, someone who dies without a will is said to have died **intestate**.

When a person dies testate, the property is distributed according to the will's provisions. If a person dies intestate, state laws of descent determine how the property is distributed among heirs or next of kin.

The court responsible for administering legal issues related to a will is called a **probate court**.

#### Testamentary Capacity and Intent

A will is valid only if the testator had testamentary capacity—meaning they were of legal age and sound mind at the time it was made. The minimum legal age for executing a will in most states is eighteen years.

Example: Benjamin v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A., 305 S.W.3d 446 (Ky.Ct.App. 2010) highlights that a will of a decedent written when the person was sixteen would be invalid if the legal age for executing a will is eighteen.
:p What does testamentary capacity refer to?
??x
Testamentary capacity refers to the testator's ability to understand and comprehend their personal plan for disposing of property at the time they make the will. It includes being of legal age and having sound mind. Persons who have been declared incompetent in a legal proceeding do not meet this requirement.

For example, Marjorie Sirgo’s case showed that despite executing a second will when she had Parkinson's disease, her first will was still valid because she lacked testamentary capacity at the time it was executed.
