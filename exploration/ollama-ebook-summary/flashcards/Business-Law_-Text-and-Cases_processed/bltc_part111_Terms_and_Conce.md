#### Repudiation
Background context: Repudiation occurs when one party to a contract indicates they will not perform their obligations, thereby putting the other party in a position where they must decide whether to wait or take action.

:p Brite Images agrees to sell posters to Poster Planet but repudiates on April 1. Can Poster Planet sue Brite without waiting until May 1?
??x
Yes, Poster Planet can sue Brite without waiting for delivery. Repudiation by the seller (Brite) releases the buyer (Poster Planet) from their obligation to wait and perform under the contract.

When a party repudiates, the non-breaching party (in this case, Poster Planet) may choose to treat the other party as if they have breached the contract even before the time for performance. This allows Poster Planet to seek remedies such as damages or specific performance without having to fulfill their part of the agreement first.

Background context: Anticipatory repudiation occurs when a party indicates in advance that they will not perform their contractual obligations at the time specified for performance. This can give the non-breaching party rights similar to those available upon actual breach.

#### Warranty of Good Title

Background context: Under the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), sellers warrant that they have good and valid title to the goods sold, meaning that the transfer of title is rightful. This warranty applies primarily in sales contracts but not in lease contracts where title does not pass to the lessee.

If the buyer subsequently learns that the seller did not have a valid title to the goods purchased, the buyer can sue the seller for breach of this warranty.
:p What is the warranty of good title?
??x
The warranty of good title ensures that the seller has valid and rightful title to the goods sold. If a buyer discovers that the seller lacked valid title, they can sue for breach of this warranty.

Example: Alexis steals two iPads from Camden and sells them to Emma, who does not know that they are stolen. When Camden learns that Emma possesses these iPads, he has the right to reclaim them because Alexis breached the warranty of good title.

#### Warranty of No Liens

Background context: The second type of title warranty protects buyers and lessees who do not know about any encumbrances on the goods at the time they enter into a contract. Such encumbrances, typically called liens, can result in claims or charges against the goods. This warranty is crucial for protecting buyers from repossession by creditors with valid security interests.

Example: Henderson buys a used boat from Loring for cash. A month later, Barish proves she has a valid security interest in the boat and that Loring is in default. If Barish repossesses the boat, Henderson can recover from Loring because under UCC 2-312(1)(b), Henderson had no actual knowledge of the lien.
:p What does the warranty of no liens protect?
??x
The warranty of no liens protects buyers who unknowingly purchase goods subject to a creditor’s security interest. If a buyer has no actual knowledge of such a security interest and is legally repossessed by the creditor, they can recover from the seller for breach of this warranty.

Example: Henderson buys a used boat from Loring without knowing about Barish's valid security interest. When Barish reposesses the boat due to Loring’s default, Henderson has grounds to sue Loring under UCC 2-312(1)(b) for breaching the warranty of no liens.

#### Types of Title Warranties

Background context: The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) recognizes three types of title warranties—good title, no liens, and no infringements—that can automatically arise in sales and lease contracts. Sellers or lessors generally cannot disclaim or modify these warranties without specific language in the contract.

If a seller breaches any of these warranties, the buyer or lessee may sue for damages.
:p What are the three types of title warranties under UCC?
??x
The three types of title warranties under UCC are:
1. **Good Title**: The seller warrants they have good and valid title to the goods sold.
2. **No Liens**: The seller guarantees that there are no encumbrances or liens on the goods at the time of sale.
3. **No Infringements**: The seller ensures the sale does not infringe on any intellectual property rights.

These warranties can arise automatically in sales and lease contracts unless specifically disclaimed by the seller with appropriate language.
??x

#### Breach of Warranty and Damages

Background context: If a seller breaches a warranty, such as good title or no liens, the buyer or lessee can sue for damages. The remedies available to the buyer/lessee include monetary compensation (damages) and in some cases, rescission (cancellation) of the contract.

If the parties have not agreed to limit or modify the remedies, a breach of warranty is considered a breach of the seller's promise.
:p What can a buyer or lessee do if a seller breaches a warranty?
??x
A buyer or lessee can sue for damages if a seller breaches a warranty. The remedies available include monetary compensation and in some cases, rescission (cancellation) of the agreement.

Example: If Alexis sells stolen iPads to Emma without disclosing their theft, Camden can reclaim them from Emma because Alexis breached the warranty of good title. Emma can then recover from Alexis for the breach.

#### Summary of Warranties

Background context: Under UCC Article 2 (sales) and Article 2A (leases), several types of warranties can arise in contracts, including warranties of title, express warranties, and implied warranties. Federal law also imposes certain requirements on warranties.

Warranties impose a duty on the seller or lessor, and breaching a warranty is considered a breach of their promise.
:p What are the key types of warranties under UCC?
??x
The key types of warranties under UCC include:
1. **Title Warranties**: Good title (seller has valid title), no liens (no encumbrances on goods).
2. **Express Warranties**: Specific assurances about the quality and features.
3. **Implied Warranties**: Warranties that arise by law, such as fitness for a particular purpose.

These warranties ensure buyers and lessees are protected against various issues related to the goods purchased or leased.
