#### Mailbox Rule and E-Contracts
Background context: The mailbox rule, also known as the deposited acceptance rule, is a principle used by most courts to determine when an acceptance of an offer becomes effective. According to this rule, if the authorized mode of communication is through the mail, then an acceptance becomes valid when it is dispatched—placed in the control of the U.S. Postal Service—not when it is received by the offeror.

However, if the offer stipulates a specific time for effectiveness, that must be followed instead. The mailbox rule does not apply to instantaneous forms of communication such as face-to-face interactions, telephone calls, faxes, and usually e-mails. For electronic transactions, the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA) provides guidance on when an email acceptance becomes effective.

Example: Under UETA, if parties agree to conduct transactions electronically, then an e-mail acceptance is considered sent when it either leaves the control of the sender or is received by the recipient.
:p What does the mailbox rule state about the timing of acceptance for mailed communications?
??x
The mailbox rule states that an acceptance via mail becomes valid and a binding contract is formed when the acceptance is dispatched (placed in the control of the U.S. Postal Service), not when it is actually received by the offeror.

Explanation: This means that as soon as the offeree puts the acceptance letter into the post, the contract is considered to be formed even if the offeror has not yet received it.
x??

??x
Under UETA, an email acceptance becomes effective when it either leaves the control of the sender or is received by the recipient. This rule replaces the mailbox rule for electronic communications and ensures that agreements formed through emails are legally binding as soon as they are dispatched.
