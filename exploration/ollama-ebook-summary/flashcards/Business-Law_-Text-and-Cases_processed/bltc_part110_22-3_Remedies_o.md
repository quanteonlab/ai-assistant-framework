#### Right to Recover Damages for Nonacceptance
Background context: When a buyer or lessee repudiates a contract or wrongfully refuses to accept goods, the seller or lessor can seek damages. The usual measure of damages is the difference between the contract price and market price at the time of tender plus incidental damages.

:p What are the usual measures of damages when a buyer or lessee repudiates a contract or wrongfully refuses to accept goods?
??x
The usual measures of damages include:
1. The difference between the contract price or lease payments and the market price or lease payments at the time and place of tender.
2. Incidental damages.

For example, if a seller sells lumber for $5000 under contract but the market price is$4000, the seller can recover the difference plus any additional costs associated with the non-acceptance.
??x
The answer with detailed explanations:
When a buyer or lessee repudiates a contract or wrongfully refuses to accept goods, the seller or lessor can seek damages. The usual measure of these damages is calculated as follows:

1. **Difference in Price**: This is the primary calculation where the seller calculates the difference between the contract price and the market price at the time of tender.

   $$\text{Damages} = \text{Contract Price or Lease Payments} - \text{Market Price or Lease Payments}$$2. **Incidental Damages**: These are additional costs that arise due to the non-acceptance, such as storage fees, transportation expenses, and other related charges.

For instance, if a seller sells lumber for $5000 under contract but the market price is $4000 at the time of tender, plus there are additional incidental damages of $1000 due to storage and handling costs, the total recoverable damages would be:
   $$
   5000 - 4000 + 1000 = \$2000
   $$

If this measure is inadequate, alternative measures can include lost profits for the seller or lessor.

#### When Goods are in Transit
Background context: If a seller or lessor has delivered goods to a carrier but not yet received by the buyer or lessee, and if the buyer or lessee breaches the contract or becomes insolvent, the seller or lessor can stop the delivery of the goods still in transit.

:p What can the seller or lessor do when goods are in transit after receiving notice that the buyer or lessee is insolvent?
??x
The seller or lessor can stop the delivery of the goods in transit regardless of the quantity shipped. If the buyer or lessee is not insolvent but still in breach, the seller or lessor can only stop the goods if at least a carload, truckload, planeload, or larger shipment has been sent.
??x
The answer with detailed explanations:
If the seller or lessor learns that the buyer or lessee is insolvent, they can stop the delivery of the goods still in transit. This means that even small shipments can be halted.

However, if the breach does not result in insolvency and the quantity shipped is less than a carload (e.g., individual packages), the seller or lessor cannot stop the delivery. They must wait until the goods are received by the buyer or lessee before taking any action.

For example:
- If Timber Products ships lumber to Ortega but Ortega has filed for bankruptcy, Timber Products can stop the shipment.
- But if Ortega is only one package short and not insolvent, Timber Products cannot stop delivery unless they ship a minimum quantity such as a carload or truckload.

#### Exclusive Remedies

#### Consequential Damages
Background context: Consequential damages compensate for indirect losses resulting from a breach and are foreseeable by both parties. The UCC allows parties to limit or exclude consequential damages if such limitations are not unconscionable. For consumers, limitations on personal injury claims due to defective consumer goods may be presumed unfair.
:p How do the UCC rules handle limitations on consequential damages?
??x
The UCC permits parties to limit or exclude consequential damages as long as such exclusions are not found to be unconscionable. However, when a buyer is a consumer and the limitation involves personal injuries caused by defective goods, it is presumed to be unconscionable.
