#### Poison Pill Defense Mechanism
Background context: When a party acquires a certain proportion of a target corporation's stock, often between 15 and 20 percent, it triggers a defense mechanism that makes the takeover too expensive. This is known as the poison pill defense.

:p What is the poison pill defense mechanism?
??x
The poison pill defense mechanism allows shareholders to acquire newly issued shares if any individual acquires more than a certain threshold of the company's stock (e.g., 10 percent). If the acquiring party tries to buy more shares, it triggers the issuance of new shares, diluting its ownership interest and making the takeover too costly.

Example: Netflix used this mechanism to block billionaire investor Carl Icahn from buying more shares. If Icahn’s stake had reached 10 percent, a large number of new shares would have been issued, significantly diluting his ownership.

#### Directors’ Fiduciary Duties in Takeover Defenses
Background context: During a hostile takeover attempt, directors must balance their self-interest with their fiduciary duties to the corporation and its shareholders. Shareholders who receive a premium for their shares may sue if they believe the directors breached their fiduciary duties.

:p What is the role of directors during a hostile takeover?
??x
During a hostile takeover, directors have a fiduciary duty to act in the best interests of the company and its shareholders. This often involves using defensive tactics to resist the takeover. Directors must show that they had reasonable grounds to believe the tender offer posed a danger to the corporation’s policies and effectiveness. Their actions should be rational in response to any threat.

Example: Courts apply the business judgment rule when analyzing whether directors acted reasonably. If their actions were deemed reasonable under the circumstances, they are not liable for breaching their fiduciary duties.
