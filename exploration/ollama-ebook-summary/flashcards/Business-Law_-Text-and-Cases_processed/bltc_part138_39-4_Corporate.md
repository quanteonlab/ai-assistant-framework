:p Can Northwest Brands avoid the double taxation on its corporate income?
??x
Northwest Brands could potentially avoid the double taxation by electing S corporation status. Under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code, the corporation's profits and losses pass through to the shareholders' personal tax returns. This means that there is no separate corporate tax, and the income or loss is reported on individual shareholder tax returns.

However, this option comes with certain requirements and limitations:
1. The corporation must have only one class of stock.
2. There can be no more than 100 shareholders who are individuals, estates, or trusts (not including certain types of business entities).
3. Shareholders must be U.S. citizens or residents.

If Northwest Brands meets these criteria, it could elect S corporation status by filing Form 2553 with the IRS within 75 days after the formation of the corporation or before the due date for its first tax return (whichever is later).

Here’s a simplified outline of the steps to make this election:
1. Ensure all shareholders are individuals, estates, or trusts.
2. File Form 2553 with the IRS.
3. Report income and losses on personal tax returns.

If Northwest Brands qualifies for S corporation status, it can avoid the double taxation issue.

:p Who would be liable for the contracts made before the corporation's formal incorporation?
??x
Upon incorporating Northwest Brands, Inc., if the necessary steps to form the corporation have been completed (such as filing the articles of incorporation and securing capitalization), then the corporation itself would generally be liable for the obligations arising from the preincorporation contracts. This is because a corporate entity is distinct from its shareholders.

However, there are a few nuances:
1. **Unilateral Contracts**: If Peterson entered into contracts with Owens or Babcock in his personal capacity and did not represent himself as acting on behalf of Northwest Brands, then only Peterson would be personally liable.
2. **Bilateral Contracts**: If the corporation was an intended party to these agreements from the outset (e.g., if the purchase contract specifically named Northwest Brands, Inc.), then the corporation would be bound by those contracts.

To ensure clarity and minimize personal liability:
- It is best practice for all preincorporation actions to be documented with clear indications that they are being undertaken on behalf of the future corporation.
- The incorporators can file preliminary agreements as evidence that the intended entity was indeed the party in these transactions.

Here’s a simplified example:
```pseudocode
// Example Contract Language

This agreement is made by and between [Owens] and [Northwest Brands, Inc.], for the sale of land owned by Owens to Northwest Brands, Inc., for $20,000."
```

By ensuring that all contracts are properly documented and reflect their intended party as the corporation, liability can be clearly assigned.

#### Ultra Vires Doctrine
Background context: Oya Paka formed a corporation called Paka Corp. to sell computer services. Later, she cosigned a loan in the name of the corporation, which defaulted. The question is whether this action was within the authority granted by the incorporators.

:p Was Oya acting within her authority when she cosigned the note on behalf of Paka Corp.?
??x
Oya's action of cosigning a loan in the name of Paka Corp. would likely be considered ultra vires (beyond the powers granted by the incorporators) and therefore invalid.

Under corporate law, directors and officers generally have specific authority as outlined in the corporation’s articles of incorporation, bylaws, or resolutions adopted by the board. Without explicit authorization from these documents or a valid corporate resolution, Oya exceeded her authority.

To determine if an action is within the corporation's powers:
1. **Articles of Incorporation**: Check for any general business purposes that might allow such actions.
2. **Bylaws and Resolutions**: Review any specific resolutions adopted by the board of directors regarding borrowing or entering into financial agreements.
3. **Corporate Records**: Ensure there are no prior records indicating approval for similar transactions.

In this case, Oya’s cosigning without explicit authorization would likely be considered ultra vires, making the bank's lawsuit against Paka Corp. on these grounds potentially unmeritorious.

If such actions were to occur, it might necessitate a court ruling on whether the action was ultra vires or if there were prior approvals that Oya failed to document.

#### Piercing the Corporate Veil
Background context: Thomas Persson and Jon Nokes founded Smart Inventions, Inc., to market household consumer products. The success of their first product led to legal challenges.

The question is about the concept of piercing the corporate veil in this scenario.
:p Can the personal assets of the shareholders be reached if Smart Inventions, Inc., fails?
??x
Yes, under certain circumstances, a court may pierce the corporate veil and hold individual shareholders personally liable for the corporation’s debts or misdeeds. This is known as "piercing the corporate veil."

For piercing to occur, courts typically look at:
1. **Misuse of Corporate Entity**: The corporation has been used to perpetrate fraud or some other wrongful act.
2. **Lack of Corporate Formalities**: Failure to maintain proper corporate formalities (e.g., keeping separate financial records, holding meetings with directors).
3. **Blurred Boundaries between Personal and Business Assets**: Use of the corporation’s assets for personal purposes or failure to pay dividends when due.

Given that Smart Inventions, Inc., has been successful but also involves household consumer products which may involve regulatory compliance issues or consumer protection concerns, if there are signs of misuse or blurred boundaries (e.g., commingling of funds), shareholders’ personal assets could be at risk.

For example:
- If the corporation has failed to properly maintain separate financial accounts for business and personal use.
- If dividends were not paid when due, suggesting a lack of independent corporate existence.
- If there was fraud or misconduct by management that harmed consumers or creditors.

Courts may consider these factors in deciding whether to pierce the veil. To mitigate this risk:
1. Maintain strict separation between personal and business finances.
2. Hold regular board meetings and document decisions formally.
3. Ensure compliance with all applicable laws and regulations.

If Smart Inventions, Inc., is found to have engaged in such practices, shareholders may be held personally liable for the corporation’s debts or damages.

:p Can R&K be held liable for the torts of its employees?
??x
Yes, R&K can be held liable for the torts committed by its employee, Keith Press. The key points are:
- **Agency Theory**: Employers are generally liable for the actions of their employees while they are acting within the scope of employment.
