#### Signature Liability: Definition and Scope
Background context explaining the concept. The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) broadly defines a signature to include any name, word, mark, or symbol that is executed or adopted by a person [UCC 1–201(37), 3–401(b)]. This definition includes signatures in various forms and contexts.

The general rule under the UCC is that every party who signs a negotiable instrument, except a qualified indorser, is either primarily or secondarily liable for payment of that instrument when it comes due. A person is not liable on an instrument unless he or she has signed it personally or through an authorized representative (agent) [UCC 3–401(a)].

#### Primary Liability in Negotiable Instruments
Primary liability on a negotiable instrument is unconditional and requires the party to pay the instrument when it comes due unless they have a valid defense to payment.

Makers and acceptors are primarily liable:
- **Makers** of promissory notes unconditionally promise to pay according to their terms.
- **Acceptors** promise to pay an instrument when it is presented later for payment.

:p Who is primarily liable on a negotiable instrument?
??x
Primary liability falls on makers and acceptors. Makers unconditionally promise to pay, while acceptors promise to pay upon presentation of the instrument.

#### Agent's Liability
Background context explaining the scenarios under which an agent may be held personally liable for a negotiable instrument. These scenarios include signing without indication of agency status, signing in both names, and indicating agency but failing to name the principal.
:p In what situations can an agent be personally liable for an instrument?
??x
An agent can be personally liable for a negotiable instrument in three specific situations:
1. When the agent signs their own name with no indication of agency.
2. When the agent signs both names (own and principal's) but nothing indicates the agency relationship.
3. When the agent indicates agency status but fails to name the principal [UCC 3-402(b)(2)].

To avoid personal liability, an authorized agent should disclose the identity of the principal and indicate that they are signing in a representative capacity.
