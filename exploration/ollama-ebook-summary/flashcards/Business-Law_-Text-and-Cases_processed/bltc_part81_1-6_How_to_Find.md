#### Natural Law School
An age-old question in law is whether citizens must obey a "bad" law. The natural law school addresses this by considering the inherent morality or righteousness of laws themselves, suggesting that certain laws may be inherently unjust and not obligatory to follow if they violate fundamental moral principles.
:p What does the natural law school address?
??x
The natural law school addresses the question of whether citizens must obey a "bad" law. It suggests that laws which are fundamentally unjust or immoral do not have to be followed, emphasizing inherent moral standards over written legal rules.

Background context: Most decisions reached by reviewing or appellate courts are explained in written opinions, which contain the court’s reasons for its decision, the rules of law that apply, and the judgment. There can be several types of opinions depending on the agreement among judges.

#### Federal Powers
Background context explaining the concept. The U.S. Constitution grants specific powers to the federal government, along with implied powers necessary to carry out those explicitly listed functions. All other powers not delegated by the Constitution to the federal government are reserved to the states or the people under the Tenth Amendment.
:p What are the two types of powers held by the federal government?
??x
